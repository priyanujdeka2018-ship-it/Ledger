Role Decode

What this role actually is — beyond the JD language

The Role in Plain English

"Strategy analyst + process designer for fraud claims — the team that decides who really pays when fraud happens."

Within AmEx's Global Fraud & Banking Risk org, the fraud claims team owns the question: "Was this transaction really fraud, or is someone gaming the system?" When a cardholder files a fraud claim, AmEx needs to determine if it's legitimate (real fraud) or suspect/bogus (the cardholder actually made the transaction). This role designs the strategies, data signals, and processes that make that determination at scale.

You are not the person reviewing individual claims. You're the person building the machine — the rules, models, and review queues — that the operations team uses to adjudicate claims. You partner with data science (who build the ML models), technology (who build the tooling), and servicing ops (who execute the reviews).

Org Context

Global Fraud & Banking Risk → Fraud Claims sub-team. Enterprise partners include: GMNS (your highest-fit AmEx target), Global Servicing, EDMX, Enterprise Digital, Technology, US Consumer & Commercial.

The team's north star: reduce fraud losses from bogus claims while maintaining the world-class customer experience AmEx is known for. Same fraud–CX tradeoff as the Uber role, different domain.

The core tension: Every bogus claim you catch saves AmEx money. Every legitimate claim you wrongly flag as bogus damages customer trust. You optimize this tradeoff with data, not intuition.

This is the headline deliverable. You build the rules, thresholds, and review queues that flag potentially bogus claims before they're paid out. Two things here: (a) pattern analysis on claims data to identify emerging fraud vectors or bogus-claim patterns, and (b) chargeback optimization — making sure AmEx recovers money from merchants when the liability sits with them. You design the operational playbook — how claims are triaged, reviewed, escalated, and resolved. Then partner with ops to make sure it actually works at volume. Your analog: Salesforce 0→1 build — creating the operational platform from scratch, then rolling it out across two geographies.

You're the strategy voice; the modeling team builds the ML. P-FC1: Suspect Claims Pattern Detector
Claude APIPythonpandasStreamlit

The Problem: Claims analysts review individual claims in isolation. Patterns across claims — repeat claimants, merchant clusters, timing patterns, amount thresholds — require structured analysis that's time-intensive to do manually.

The Build: A Streamlit dashboard that ingests a mock claims dataset (synthetic data), uses Claude API to identify patterns across multiple dimensions (claimant behavior, merchant patterns, temporal signals), and outputs a structured risk-priority queue. Each flagged pattern includes: the signal detected, the confidence level, the suggested review action, and the estimated loss at risk.

The Pitch: "Instead of reviewing 1,000 claims linearly, the tool surfaces the 50 that have the highest bogus-probability signal — and explains why. The analyst makes the judgment call; the AI does the pattern-matching at scale."

Build time: 1 weekend. Data: Synthetic claims dataset (generate from public fraud statistics).

P-FC2: Chargeback Recovery Optimiser
Claude APIPythonJSON schemaStreamlit

The Problem: Chargeback representment (recovering money from merchants) requires analyzing dispute evidence against card-network rules. Missed representments = lost recovery. Over-represented cases = wasted ops time.

The Build: A tool where you input a dispute case summary and it outputs: (a) the applicable reason codes, (b) the evidence required for successful representment, (c) a win-probability score based on case characteristics, and (d) a recommended action (represent, accept, or escalate). Trained on public chargeback reason-code documentation from Mastercard and Visa.

The Pitch: "The JD says 'validate that chargeback opportunities are fully optimised.' This tool ensures no recoverable case falls through the cracks — and no unwinnable case wastes ops resources."

Build time: 1 weekend. Data: Public reason-code documentation + synthetic case data.

P-FC3: Fraud Claims KPI Report Generator
PythonpandasClaude APIMarkdown

The Problem: Daily/weekly/monthly KPI reporting for fraud claims is repetitive assembly work — same structure, same metrics, different numbers. Analyst time spent on deck assembly is time not spent on pattern analysis.

The Build: A Python script that takes weekly mock CSV exports (claim volumes, bogus rates, recovery rates, TAT metrics) and outputs a structured KPI report — trends, callouts, anomalies flagged, recommended actions. Claude API drafts the narrative; human reviews and approves.

The Pitch: "Redirect analyst time from report assembly to actual fraud judgment. The JD requires daily/weekly/monthly reporting — this tool makes that cadence sustainable without burning analyst capacity on formatting."

The Roles, Decoded

Two roles, same team, complementary mandates — what GCG actually wants from each one

The GCG Context

The Global Client Group within American Express's GMNS Business Unit serves AmEx's largest and most complex global merchants across retail, online, and travel — representing $300B+ in charge volume. The mission: drive revenue through premium service, high-touch engagement, and being the merchant's partner of choice for global commerce.

These two newly-positioned Business Operations roles sit at the intersection of GCG's commercial mandate and AmEx's enterprise risk discipline. They exist because as GCG scales relationships across millions in merchant volume, the operational risk surface scales with it — and AmEx wants it managed with the same rigor as its consumer card business. Risk discipline is not a brake on commercial outcomes; it is the infrastructure that protects them.

These are paired roles, not duplicate ones. Think of them as a leadership tandem running GCG's risk operations stack:

They want a leader who can hold risk accountability for an entire global organization, represent GCG in enterprise forums, and translate ambiguous risk mandates into executable change across CLMs, Tech, Compliance, and GCO.

The Unstated Requirements (Director)
  • Walk into a vacuum and own it: "Newly created" means no precedent. You'll be writing the operating model from scratch in the first 90 days. AmEx wants someone who is energized by this, not destabilized.
  • Influence without authority across $300B in volume stakes: No CLM reports to you. No Tech team reports to you. Yet every ORE you own touches their work. Trust capital is your only currency.
  • Decisive in ambiguity: "Customer experience is protected" requires you to call it when something is unsafe — even when consensus is missing. The Director who waits for full alignment will be the one who lets a control gap escape.
  • Enterprise fluency: Guardian, GCO, Internal Audit — these are not just acronyms. They are political ecosystems with their own incentives. You need to navigate without being captured by any of them.
  • Hands-on at scale: "Hands-on leadership style with high accountability and follow-through" — this is not a strategy role. The JD repeats execution language. AmEx wants a leader who reviews the ORE register personally on a Friday afternoon.

Behavioural Coaching — Three Conscious Activations for Interview Day

Your DISC profile (High C, High S, Low D, Low I) and CSF (no Influencing themes in Top 5) means three behaviours that are not your default need to be on intentionally during interview rounds:

  1. Lead with the answer, not the analysis. Your Restorative + Analytical instinct is to walk through the diagnostic path. Senior interviewers want the conclusion first, then the reasoning. Practice the inversion: "I held the threshold. Here's why."
  2. Use ownership language, not consultative language. Replace "we decided" with "I decided" in stories where you led. Replace "I worked with the CCO" with "I escalated to the CCO and recommended X — they approved." The action belongs to you.

Note: These are interview-room activations, not personality changes. Your steadiness is what AmEx wants in the role — it's the visibility of that steadiness in a 60-minute window that needs deliberate practice.

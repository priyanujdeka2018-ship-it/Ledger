Qualcomm PM — Domain Vocabulary
Words That Must Sound Natural in Your Interview
Plan of Record (PoR)
The official, agreed-upon baseline plan for a programme — schedule, budget, deliverables, resources. Any deviation from PoR is a formal event requiring escalation.
Your equivalent: Monthly AED 17.7B collections PoR tracked against daily actuals in governance reports.
RAID Log
Risks, Actions, Issues, Decisions — the PM's central programme health tracker. A living document updated every sprint/week.
Your equivalent: Your 9 governance reports covering SLA, AR ageing, escalations, and corrective actions.
Milestone Gate Review
A formal checkpoint at which programme deliverables are assessed before the next phase begins. Qualcomm calls these M0, M1, M2... through tape-out.
Your equivalent: Monthly MD-level governance forum where you present collections PoR status and corrective actions.
SoC (System on Chip)
A single integrated circuit with all necessary components — CPU, GPU, modem, memory controller. Snapdragon is a Qualcomm SoC. As PM, you don't design it — you manage the delivery programme for it.
—
Tape-Out
The final step before a chip goes to manufacturing — the design files are "taped out" to the foundry (TSMC, Samsung). Missing tape-out is a programme failure with tens of millions in impact. Everything a Qualcomm PM does is ultimately about protecting tape-out date.
Analogy: Your AED 8.2B dues collections year-end target. Everything you track protects it.
IP Block / IP Integration
Reusable circuit design components ("intellectual property blocks") that are integrated into larger chip designs. PM manages delivery timelines for each IP block from multiple engineering teams.
Analogy: Individual programme workstreams in your 12-concurrent-deliverable model.
RTL (Register Transfer Level)
The stage of chip design where logic is written in HDL code. RTL freeze is a key milestone — after this, design changes are expensive.
Analogy: Like your MD-approved process documents — once signed, changes require formal re-approval.
Verification / Validation
In chip engineering: Verification = checking that the design does what the spec says. Validation = checking the real silicon works in real conditions. PM tracks both milestones.
Analogy: Your QCG (Quality Control) function tracking collections process compliance.
NPI (New Product Introduction)
The full process of taking a new product from concept to market availability. NPI programme management is a distinct PM discipline at Qualcomm — managing cross-functional readiness for a new chip launch.
Analogy: Your entity expansion programme — applying Sobha's collections framework to Siniya Island and Downtown UAQ.
Sprint / Velocity / Burndown
Agile delivery concepts: Sprint = 2-week work cycle. Velocity = how much work a team completes per sprint. Burndown = chart showing remaining work vs. time. PMI-ACP covers these in detail.
Your equivalent: Weekly collections milestone tracking — progress vs. target, velocity of receipts.
Critical Path
The sequence of dependent tasks that determines the minimum programme duration. A delay on the critical path delays the entire programme. PM must know and protect the critical path at all times.
Your equivalent: Your 55-day critical collection window (T−25 to T+30) — the sequence that determines month-end outcome.
Scope Creep
Uncontrolled expansion of programme scope without corresponding adjustments to timeline, budget, or resources. Managing scope creep is a core PM discipline — requires formal change control.
Your equivalent: Collections scope expanding from Sobha → Siniya → UAQ — you built replication frameworks to absorb this without breaking the core programme.

AI Product Management — Core Vocabulary
Words That Must Sound Natural in Your Microsoft Interview
Agentic AI / AI Agents
AI systems that can autonomously take sequences of actions — perceive, plan, act, and adapt — without constant human instruction. CIP uses agents to automatically detect quality issues across Bing/Edge/Copilot.
Your analogy: Your automated incentive calculation engine — rule-based automation as the non-AI precursor to an agentic system.
Model Evaluation / Evals
The process of measuring how well an AI model performs against defined benchmarks. For CIP, "evals" might include accuracy of issue detection, false positive rate, and coverage across product surfaces. As PM, you define the success metrics for the AI model.
Your analogy: Your quality scorecard — defining what "good quality" looks like and measuring it systematically.
OKR (Objectives & Key Results)
Microsoft's primary goal framework. Objective = qualitative aspiration. Key Result = measurable outcome. CIP's OKRs might include: "Detect 90% of P0 quality issues before user exposure" with KRs around detection rate, time-to-detect, and coverage breadth.
Your equivalent: Your first-ever Annual Goals framework for Collections — you built OKRs without calling them OKRs.
Responsible AI / RAI
Microsoft's framework for building AI that is fair, reliable, safe, private, inclusive, transparent, and accountable. CIP PM must apply RAI principles: ensuring AI quality testing doesn't introduce bias, maintains data privacy, and keeps humans appropriately in the loop.
Your analogy: Your process governance documents — formalising how decisions are made and who is accountable.
North Star Metric
The single most important metric that captures the essence of the product's value delivery. For CIP, the North Star might be "% of quality issues caught before user exposure." Everything else is an input metric that drives the North Star.
Your equivalent: Delinquency rate (16.7% → 4.4%) — the single metric that captured portfolio health, driving all collection programme decisions.
PRD (Product Requirements Document)
The document that defines what a product feature should do, why, for whom, and how success is measured. Microsoft PMs write PRDs that drive engineering sprint planning. Equivalent to: problem statement + user story + acceptance criteria + success metrics.
Your equivalent: Your 6 MD-approved process documents — structured requirements with owner sign-off and measurable acceptance criteria.
User Story / Acceptance Criteria
User Story: "As a [Bing engineer], I want [to see issues flagged before code merge], so that [I don't ship regressions]." Acceptance Criteria: the specific conditions the feature must meet to be considered done. PMs write these; engineers build to them.
Your equivalent: Your feature requirements for Sobha One App payments — you defined what the payment flow needed to do for customers.
Product-Market Fit (Internal)
For an internal platform like CIP, PMF = teams genuinely adopting it because it solves a real pain, not because they're mandated to. Measured by: retention (are teams still using it after month 3?), NPS, and expansion (are they using more features over time?).
Your equivalent: Sobha One App payment adoption — 0 to 278 transactions in year 1, measuring real voluntary uptake.
Experimentation / A/B Testing
Running controlled experiments to measure the causal impact of a product change. Microsoft runs thousands of A/B experiments across Bing/Edge/Copilot. CIP's quality testing may itself be validated through experiments: "Did teams using CIP ship 30% fewer P0 bugs than teams not using it?"
Your equivalent: Your NPV model for the advance rebate policy — you modelled the impact of a policy change before committing to it.
Compute / Inference Cost
The cost of running AI models in production — every API call, every LLM inference, every agentic task costs money. CIP PM must balance capability (more AI) against cost (more compute spend). Budget management in this role is largely about compute cost optimisation.
Your equivalent: Your Azure SQL licensing evaluation — should I buy this, what does it cost, what do I get?
Crowdsourcing / Crowd Intelligence
Using large numbers of people (crowd) to perform tasks AI alone can't do well — labelling data, validating AI outputs, testing edge cases. "CIP" = Crowd Intelligence Platform — it uses both AI agents AND human crowd signals to detect quality issues. PM must balance AI automation with human-in-the-loop validation.
Your equivalent: Your 12 Dues RMs as a "crowd" feeding real customer data signals into your collections intelligence system.
Platform vs. Point Solution
Platform: reusable infrastructure that many teams build on (CIP). Point Solution: a specific tool built for one use case. CIP's PM must think platform-first — every new capability should be reusable by multiple MAI teams, not just one.
Your equivalent: Your Salesforce report infrastructure — built once, used by 40+ RMs, MD, and CRO. That's platform thinking.

Microsoft PM Case Study Framework

Microsoft PM Case Template (Live in 1-Hour Round)

Microsoft will give you a case like: "Design a feature for CIP to improve issue detection coverage for Copilot experiences." Use this framework:

  • Clarify the goal: "Before I design, I want to make sure I understand what success looks like — is the goal detection coverage, detection speed, or detection accuracy?"
  • Define the user: "Who is the primary user — the Copilot engineer, the QA lead, or the product manager?"
  • Map the pain: "What does the workflow look like today without this feature? Where is the breakdown?"
  • Prioritise solutions: Use a 2x2 (Impact vs. Effort). Pick the top-right quadrant. Explain your reasoning.
  • Define success metrics: North Star + 2–3 input metrics. Include guardrail metrics (what should NOT get worse).
  • Trade-offs: "The risk with this approach is [X]. Here's how I'd mitigate it."

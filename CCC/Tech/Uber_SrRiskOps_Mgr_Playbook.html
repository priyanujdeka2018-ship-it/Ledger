The Role, Decoded

Uber for Business (U4B) is Uber's B2B arm — corporate travel, employee commute, meal programs, courier services sold to enterprises. Financial fraud on U4B is structurally different from consumer fraud: higher per-transaction values, invoice fraud, expense fraud, account takeover on corporate accounts, synthetic employee profiles. This role exists because U4B fraud losses and false-positive friction are both meaningful line items — and the business needs someone to own the tradeoff.

The core tension of this role: every control you tighten reduces fraud loss but also blocks legitimate corporate customers. Every control you loosen the other way. Your job is to find the curve — with data.

Role Translated Into Plain English

"COO of Risk & Controls for Uber for Business"
Role Essence

You own the operational side of fraud strategy for U4B — not the ML models (those sit with Data Science), not the policy (that sits with Risk Leadership), but everything that happens between the signal firing and the customer experience resolving. That includes: designing rules and tests, running queue operations, managing false-positive recovery, presenting loss/CX tradeoffs to leadership, and scaling processes as U4B grows.

Case Study Prep — The Likely Case

Expected format: "Here's a dataset of U4B transactions flagged by 3 fraud rules. Some were confirmed fraud, some were false positives. Propose a strategy to improve the FPR–recall tradeoff."

Framework to use (memorize):

  1. Diagnose first: What's the current FPR, precision, recall by rule? What's the loss rate?
  2. Segment: Are false positives clustered by customer segment, transaction type, rule?
  3. Hypothesize: Which rule is over-firing? Why?
  4. Propose: Rule tightening/loosening, threshold change, new signal, ML model swap
  5. Test plan: Holdout group, shadow mode, primary metric (fraud loss $), guardrail (FPR, chargeback recovery TAT)
  6. Rollout & monitor: Staged rollout %, weekly review cadence, rollback criteria
  7. Communicate: To Product (CX impact), to Finance (loss impact), to Ops (queue volume impact)
